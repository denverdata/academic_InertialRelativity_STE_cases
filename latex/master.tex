\section{Introduction}
This paper compares the relative pace of time between two uniform spherical systems using only their masses and radii. The DeGerlia compactness $D = M/R$ \citep{degerlia202505} is the relevant property, and the ratio of two systems' compactness values gives the ratio of their paces of time.

When one system is compared with itself at its Schwarzschild radius, this ratio is the standard gravitational time-dilation calculation \citep{einstein1915,schwarzschild1916radius}. When two systems share a radius and differ only in mass, the ratio is the standard Lorentz time-dilation calculation \citep{einstein1905}. The six worked cases in Section~\ref{sec:expressions} and the appendix tables (Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}) demonstrate agreement with the standard general-relativistic calculation across the classical and relativistic regimes.
\section{Inertial Relativity}\label{sec:inertial_relativity}
Inertia is relativistic, meaning that without a comparator, any measurement of it would have no meaning. For example, something with a moment of inertia $1 \times 10^{20}$ kg\,m$^2$ might be perceived as difficult to rotate, but not more difficult than something with $1 \times 10^{30}$ kg\,m$^2$. However, the concept of relativity also says that how something is observed is influenced by the frame of observation.
A system, in this context, is any collection of mass and spatial extent one chooses to define. It need not be spherical, uniform, high density, or even massive. A system may be a planet, a uniform sphere, a supermassive black hole, a disc galaxy, a cube, a cubic sample of a cumulus cloud, or a system consisting of a hydrogen proton and a single electron.

\subsection{Rotational Inertia}
Moment of inertia dictates how an applied torque translates into angular acceleration. It is defined as:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:moi}
I = \sum_i m_i r_i^2
\end{equation}
where $r_i$ is the perpendicular distance from each mass element $m_i$ to the rotation axis. Just as mass governs how a system responds to linear force ($F = ma$), moment of inertia governs how it responds to torque ($\tau = I\alpha$).

\subsection{Linear Inertia}
Linear inertia is mass:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:mass}
M = \sum_i m_i
\end{equation}
where $m_i$ are the constituent mass elements. Linear inertia defines how an applied force translates into translational acceleration. It is quantified entirely by mass ($F = ma$). It is dimensional, operating along a specific spatial vector, but unlike moment of inertia, it is isotropic: mass offers the same resistance to changes in motion regardless of the direction of the vector.

\subsection{Isometric Scaling}
Isometric scaling is the uniform scaling of a system in all spatial dimensions while maintaining constant density. Under isometric scaling, every length in the system scales by the same factor $k$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:length_scaling}
\ell' = k\ell
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is the characteristic length of the system and $k$ is the linear scale factor. Isometric scaling of a system by a factor of $k$ results in the following changes to mass, radius, and moment of inertia:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:mass_scaling}
M' = k^3 M
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the mass and $k$ is the linear scale factor.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:radius_scaling}
R' = kR
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the radius.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:inertia_scaling}
I' = k^5 I
\end{equation}
where $I$ is the moment of inertia and $k$ is the linear scale factor.

\subsection{Inertia Abstracts Mass and Radius}
Because every change in length necessarily changes the system's mass, Equations~\ref{eq:length_scaling}--\ref{eq:radius_scaling} hold only for strict isometric scaling; Equation~\ref{eq:inertia_scaling}, however, abstracts mass and radius into moment of inertia. If two systems share moment of inertia, then an identical torque applied to each system will produce identical changes in motion, regardless of the system geometry; independent of mass and radius individually. The moment of inertia alone dictates how a system interacts with time. From this, we can conclude, for all systems that share that same density, regardless of geometry the following formula is universal and is not bound in any way to isometric scaling. The derivations that follow demonstrate that General and Special Relativity emerge from this principle.

\subsection{Universality}
For any two uniform spherical systems, the characteristic length scale factor that determines time dilation is the fifth root of the inverse ratio of their moments of inertia, multiplied by the ratio of their densities to the $3/10$ power (Equation~\ref{eq:ste_density}). Stated more cleanly, it is the ratio of the respective DeGerlia compactness $D$ values for the compared systems (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}).

\section{Inertial Density $P$}\label{sec:inertial_density}
Inertial density $P$ \citep{degerlia202505} is the moment of inertia of a system divided by its volume:
\begin{equation}
P = I/V
\end{equation}
Since linear inertia is mass, this reduces directly to mass density $\rho = M/V$ in the linear case; inertial density is the rotational generalization of mass density, a quantity already familiar from linear mechanics. Or more accurately, mass density is simply the linear interpretation of inertial density. 

Substituting $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$ and $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$, with $R$ the radius, this reduces to:
\begin{equation}
P = \frac{M}{R} \times \frac{3}{10\pi}
\end{equation}
Just as ordinary density ($\rho = M/V$) is mass distributed over volume, inertial density is rotational inertia distributed over volume. As stated in the prior work: \textit{mass and density are to linear motion as moment of inertia and inertial density are to rotational motion.}

\subsection{DeGerlia Compactness $D$}
$D = M/R$ is a simplified analog to inertial density, related to $P$ by the geometric factor $3/10\pi$, and represents the mass over radius ratio for a system. Given that this quantity is almost always used in a ratio that would cancel out all geometric vectors and constants, for ease of use, this metric $D$ for inertial density is typically used over the geometrically accurate inertial density, $P$. 



\subsection{DeGerlia Threshold $D_{crit}$}

The Schwarzschild condition can be expressed as a static universal constant threshold of DeGerlia compactness $D$. There are several advantages to this approach, not the least of which is that you don't have to calculate the threshold for every mass. And, it's much easier to calculate $D$ than $P$. inertial density is an intuitive metric that's easier to work with. 

Setting $R = r_s$ (the Schwarzschild condition) \citep{schwarzschild1916radius} and solving yields the \textbf{DeGerlia Threshold}: the universal constant of DeGerlia compactness at which the escape velocity equals the speed of light:
\begin{equation}
D_{crit} = \frac{c^2}{2G} = (6.73295 \pm 0.00015) \times 10^{26} \text{ kg/m}
\end{equation}
Note this value is exact and accurate to the limit of precision imposed by $G$, which is accurate to six significant figures with a margin of error of $15/10^6$ \citep{codata2018}. 

\section{The Space-Time Equivalence}
The following identity relates the time-dilation ratio of two uniform spherical systems to their mass and radius.\footnote{This identity has been previously referred to as the ``space-time equivalence''~\cite{degerlia202501}.}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:kd}
k_d = \frac{I_1}{I_2} \cdot \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^3 = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1}
\end{equation}
where $I_1, I_2$ are the moments of inertia, $R_1, R_2$ are the radii, $M_1, M_2$ are the masses, and $D_i = M_i/R_i$ is the DeGerlia Compactness of system $i$. The three forms in Equation~\ref{eq:kd} are algebraically identical; $(I_1/I_2)(R_2/R_1)^3$ exposes the inertia-radius structure, $D_1/D_2$ exposes the compactness-ratio structure, and $M_1 R_2/(M_2 R_1)$ is the fully expanded form.

The pace of time has meaning only relative to another pace of time. The compactness ratio $k_d$ governs the relative time-dilation between two uniform spherical systems. The standard gravitational time-dilation calculation for a single system takes the Schwarzschild form $\sqrt{1 - k_s}$, where $k_s = D/D_{crit} = r_s/R$. The compactness ratio $k_d = D_1/D_2$ and the Schwarzschild form $k_s$ are the same scale factor $k$ evaluated by two routes; the subscript denotes which route produced it.

Under isometric scaling (constant density), the isometric equivalence holds:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:isometric}
k_i = \left(\frac{I_1}{I_2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
where $k_i$ is the linear scale factor derived from the inertia ratio. Under isometric conditions the two systems share the same density, and the fifth root of the inertia ratio recovers the linear scale factor exactly. Subsequent references to this specific inertia-derived form use $k_i$.

In the linear context, inertia is $M$ and the linear inertia version of the isometric equivalence applies (see Section~\ref{sec:expressions}). Special Relativity describes linear inertia:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:linear}
k_m = \left(\frac{M_1}{M_2}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}
where $M_1$ and $M_2$ are the masses of the two systems and $k_m$ is the linear scale factor derived from the mass ratio. Subsequent references to this specific mass-derived form use $k_m$.

Via the Schwarzschild radius, the same scale factor is:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ks}
k_s = \frac{D}{D_{crit}} = \frac{r_s}{R}
\end{equation}
where $D = M/R$ is the system's DeGerlia Compactness and $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ is its Schwarzschild radius. $k_s$ is the Schwarzschild-route name for $k$ that appears in the standard $\sqrt{1 - k}$ for GTD.

\subsection{Relating Linear Scale Factor $k$ to General Relativity}
Time dilation is always:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:td_k}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - k}
\end{equation}
where $d\tau/dt$ is the ratio of proper time to coordinate time and $k$ is the compactness ratio for the case being computed. The specific forms are:
\begin{itemize}
\item $k_d = D_1/D_2$ (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}) --- via inertial density ratio
\item $k_s = D/D_{crit} = r_s/R$ (Equation~\ref{eq:ks}) --- via Schwarzschild radius
\item $k_i = (I_1/I_2)^{1/5}$ (Equation~\ref{eq:isometric}) --- via moment of inertia ratio (constant density)
\item $k_m = (M_1/M_2)^{1/3}$ (Equation~\ref{eq:linear}) --- via mass ratio (static radius)
\end{itemize}
Same $k$ symbol across forms: a given numerical $k$ produces the same time-scale-factor result regardless of which form it appears in.

\subsection{STE Example}

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Setup.} A uniform sphere of mass $1$\,kg and radius $1$\,m (System~2) is scaled isometrically by a factor of two, producing a sphere of mass $2^3 = 8$\,kg and radius $2$\,m (System~1). The linear scale factor between them is $k = R_1/R_2 = 2$ by construction.

\textbf{Change in inertia.} The moments of inertia are
\[
I_1 = \tfrac{2}{5}(8)(2)^2 = 12.8\,\text{kg\,m}^2, \qquad
I_2 = \tfrac{2}{5}(1)(1)^2 = 0.4\,\text{kg\,m}^2,
\]
so $I_1/I_2 = 32 = 2^5 = k^5$ --- the moment of inertia changes by the fifth power of the linear scale factor.

\textbf{Gravitational time dilation.} Each system's Schwarzschild radius is $r_s = 2GM/c^2$, giving $r_{s,1} = 1.18819 \times 10^{-26}$\,m and $r_{s,2} = 1.48523 \times 10^{-27}$\,m. The Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation $gtd = \sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$ is then
\begin{gather*}
gtd_1 = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1}} = 1 - 2.97046 \times 10^{-27}, \\
gtd_2 = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,2}}{R_2}} = 1 - 7.42616 \times 10^{-28},
\end{gather*}
so the relative time dilation between the two systems is
\[
\frac{gtd_1}{gtd_2} = 1 - 2.22785 \times 10^{-27}.
\]

\textbf{Compactness ratio.} The compactness ratio follows from the inertial densities $D = M/R$ ($D_1 = 4$\,kg/m, $D_2 = 1$\,kg/m):
\[
k_d = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{4}{1} = 4 = k^2, \qquad \sqrt{k_d} = 2 = k.
\]

This matches the STE Example column of Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}: the gravitational time dilation ratio is $1 - 2.22785 \times 10^{-27}$ and $\sqrt{k_d} = 2$.

\subsection{Density-Explicit Form}

The STE (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}) can be expressed equivalently in terms of density. With $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$ and $M = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \rho$, so $I \propto \rho R^5$. Taking the fifth root of the inertia ratio ($k_i$ from Equation~\ref{eq:isometric}) gives:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ki}
\left(\frac{I_1}{I_2}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_2}\right)^{1/5} \cdot \frac{R_1}{R_2}
\end{equation}
Substituting Equation~\ref{eq:ki} into the STE (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}) and resolving the radius ratio in terms of density yields the universal STE in $(I, \rho)$ coordinates:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ste_density}
\sqrt{k_d} = \left(\frac{I_1}{I_2}\right)^{1/5} \cdot \left(\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_2}\right)^{3/10}
\end{equation}
The $3/10$ exponent ($= 1/2 - 1/5$) on the density ratio arises from the difference between the density dependence in $\sqrt{k_d}$ ($1/2$ power) and the inertia ratio (Equation~\ref{eq:isometric}, $1/5$ power). Under constant density this factor is 1 and $k_i$ (Equation~\ref{eq:isometric}) gives the compactness ratio directly. Equation~\ref{eq:ste_density} is the universal STE; $k_i$ is its constant-density case. The pair comparison tables (Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}) demonstrate the universal form's necessity in the non-isometric cases, where the density correction is not 1.

\section{General and Special Relativity}
Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:schwarzschild}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{R}}
\end{equation}
where $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild radius and $R$ is the radius.

Lorentz time dilation:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:lorentz}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}
\end{equation}
where $v$ is the velocity of the moving frame and $c$ is the speed of light.

These are the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, where $k = r_s/R$ in the gravitational case and $k = v^2/c^2$ in the velocity case. The two expressions are equivalent: the escape velocity at radius $R$ is $v^2 = 2GM/R$ \citep{goldstein2002}, so $v^2/c^2 = 2GM/(Rc^2) = r_s/R$.

Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation is the STE (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}) evaluated under the constraint $M_1 = M_2$ (same mass, different radii). Lorentz time dilation is the STE evaluated under the constraint $R_1 = R_2$ (same radius, different masses). Both are cases of the same compactness-based relation; the derivations of each are developed in Section~\ref{sec:expressions}.


\section{Expressions of Relative Inertial Linear Scale}\label{sec:expressions}

The universal scale factor between any two systems is the compactness ratio $k_d$ (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}), with $k_d = D_1/D_2 = M_1 R_2/(M_2 R_1)$. Each subsection below applies a specific physical constraint that simplifies $k_d$, derives the resulting form, and demonstrates the calculation with a worked numerical example.

Numerical values are reported at six significant figures in e-style notation. Reference constants used throughout: $D_{crit} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26}$ kg/m, $G = 6.67430 \times 10^{-11}$ m$^3$\,kg$^{-1}$\,s$^{-2}$, $c = 2.99792 \times 10^{8}$ m/s. Example parameter sets match the columns of the pair comparison tables (Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}).

\subsection{Static Mass (Gravitational) ($M_1 = M_2$)}

When the two systems share the same mass, the mass terms cancel in $k_d$:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{R_2}{R_1}
\]
The compactness ratio reduces to a ratio of radii. Equivalently in terms of DeGerlia compactness $D = M/R$, this is $D_1/D_2 = R_2/R_1$; each system's normalized DeGerlia compactness against the DeGerlia Threshold yields $D/D_{crit} = r_s/R$, recovering the Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation parameter.

This case is the standard gravitational time-dilation calculation; the $M_1 R_2/(M_2 R_1)$ ratio reduces to the familiar Schwarzschild factor here.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Static Mass):} $S_1$: $M_1 = 2.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_1 = 3.00000 \times 10^{3}$\,m (near the Schwarzschild radius). $S_2$: $M_2 = 2.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_2 = 7.00000 \times 10^{8}$\,m.

Compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{R_2}{R_1} = \frac{7.00000 \times 10^{8}}{3.00000 \times 10^{3}} = 2.33333 \times 10^{5}
\]

\textbf{GTD calculation:} Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation directly. With $M_1 = M_2$, both systems share the same Schwarzschild radius:
\begin{align}
r_{s,1} = r_{s,2} &= \frac{2GM}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(2.00000 \times 10^{30})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 2.97046 \times 10^{3}\,\text{m} \notag
\end{align}
GTD for each system via $\sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$:
\begin{align}
\text{GTD}_1 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{2.97046 \times 10^{3}}{3.00000 \times 10^{3}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 9.90155 \times 10^{-1}} = 1 - 9.00777 \times 10^{-1} \notag \\
\text{GTD}_2 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,2}}{R_2}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{2.97046 \times 10^{3}}{7.00000 \times 10^{8}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 4.24352 \times 10^{-6}} = 1 - 2.12176 \times 10^{-6} \notag
\end{align}
giving the relative pace of time:
\[
\frac{\text{GTD}_1}{\text{GTD}_2} = \frac{1 - 9.00777 \times 10^{-1}}{1 - 2.12176 \times 10^{-6}} = 1 - 9.00776 \times 10^{-1}
\]
\end{mdframed}

\noindent See Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $M = M$.

\subsection{Static Radius (Linear) ($R_1 = R_2$)}

When the two systems share the same radius, the radius terms cancel in $k_d$:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{M_1}{M_2}
\]
The compactness ratio reduces to a ratio of masses. The Lorentz factor (Equation~\ref{eq:lorentz}) has the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, with $k = v^2/c^2$. Since velocity is distance per unit time, $v^2/c^2$ is the square of a ratio of two lengths, with the shared time unit cancelling. Gravitational time dilation confirms this directly: $v^2/c^2 = r_s/R$ via the escape velocity identity, so the velocity-based $k$ of special relativity and the radius-based $k$ of general relativity are the same length ratio.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Static Radius):} $S_1$: $M_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $S_2$: $M_2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{24}$\,kg, both at $R = 1.00000 \times 10^{8}$\,m.

Compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1}{M_2} = \frac{1.00000 \times 10^{30}}{1.00000 \times 10^{24}} = 1.00000 \times 10^{6}
\]

\textbf{GTD calculation:} Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation directly. Each system's Schwarzschild radius is $r_s = 2GM/c^2$:
\begin{align}
r_{s,1} &= \frac{2GM_1}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{30})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{3}\,\text{m} \notag \\
r_{s,2} &= \frac{2GM_2}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{24})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m} \notag
\end{align}
GTD for each system via $\sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$:
\begin{align}
\text{GTD}_1 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,1}}{R}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{3}}{1.00000 \times 10^{8}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-5}} = 1 - 7.42619 \times 10^{-6} \notag \\
\text{GTD}_2 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,2}}{R}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{-3}}{1.00000 \times 10^{8}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-11}} = 1 - 7.42616 \times 10^{-12} \notag
\end{align}
giving the relative pace of time:
\[
\frac{\text{GTD}_1}{\text{GTD}_2} = \frac{1 - 7.42619 \times 10^{-6}}{1 - 7.42616 \times 10^{-12}} = 1 - 7.42618 \times 10^{-6}
\]
\end{mdframed}

\noindent See Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $R = R$.

\subsection{Constant Density (Isometric) ($\rho_1 = \rho_2$)}

When the two systems share the same density, $M \propto R^3$, so $M_1/M_2 = (R_1/R_2)^3$. Substituting into $k_d$:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \left(\frac{R_1}{R_2}\right)^3 \cdot \frac{R_2}{R_1} = \left(\frac{R_1}{R_2}\right)^2
\]
The compactness ratio reduces to the square of the radius ratio. The linear scale factor follows as $\sqrt{k_d} = R_1/R_2$, recovering the isometric equivalence.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Constant Density):} $S_1$: $M_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{8}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{27}$\,kg, $R_2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{7}$\,m. Both at $\rho = 2.38732 \times 10^{5}$\,kg/m$^3$.

Compactness ratio and linear scale factor:
\[
k_d = \left(\frac{R_1}{R_2}\right)^2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{2}, \qquad \sqrt{k_d} = \frac{R_1}{R_2} = 1.00000 \times 10^{1}
\]

\textbf{GTD calculation:} Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation directly. Each system's Schwarzschild radius is $r_s = 2GM/c^2$:
\begin{align}
r_{s,1} &= \frac{2GM_1}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{30})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{3}\,\text{m} \notag \\
r_{s,2} &= \frac{2GM_2}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{27})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{0}\,\text{m} \notag
\end{align}
GTD for each system via $\sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$:
\begin{align}
\text{GTD}_1 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{3}}{1.00000 \times 10^{8}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-5}} = 1 - 7.42619 \times 10^{-6} \notag \\
\text{GTD}_2 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,2}}{R_2}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{0}}{1.00000 \times 10^{7}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-7}} = 1 - 7.42616 \times 10^{-8} \notag
\end{align}
giving the relative pace of time:
\[
\frac{\text{GTD}_1}{\text{GTD}_2} = \frac{1 - 7.42619 \times 10^{-6}}{1 - 7.42616 \times 10^{-8}} = 1 - 7.35193 \times 10^{-6}
\]
\end{mdframed}

\noindent See Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $\rho = \rho$.

\subsection{Constant Inertia ($I_1 = I_2$)}

When the two systems share the same moment of inertia, $M_1 R_1^2 = M_2 R_2^2$, so $M_1/M_2 = (R_2/R_1)^2$. Substituting into $k_d$:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{R_2}{R_1} = \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^3
\]
The compactness ratio reduces to the cube of the radius ratio.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Constant Inertia):} $S_1$: $M_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{32}$\,kg, $R_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{7}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{8}$\,m. Both have $I = MR^2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{46}$\,kg$\cdot$m$^2$.

Compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^3 = \left(\frac{1.00000 \times 10^{8}}{1.00000 \times 10^{7}}\right)^3 = 1.00000 \times 10^{3}
\]

\textbf{GTD calculation:} Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation directly. Each system's Schwarzschild radius is $r_s = 2GM/c^2$:
\begin{align}
r_{s,1} &= \frac{2GM_1}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{32})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{5}\,\text{m} \notag \\
r_{s,2} &= \frac{2GM_2}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{30})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{3}\,\text{m} \notag
\end{align}
GTD for each system via $\sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$:
\begin{align}
\text{GTD}_1 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{5}}{1.00000 \times 10^{7}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-2}} = 1 - 7.45394 \times 10^{-3} \notag \\
\text{GTD}_2 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,2}}{R_2}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{3}}{1.00000 \times 10^{8}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-5}} = 1 - 7.42619 \times 10^{-6} \notag
\end{align}
giving the relative pace of time:
\[
\frac{\text{GTD}_1}{\text{GTD}_2} = \frac{1 - 7.45394 \times 10^{-3}}{1 - 7.42619 \times 10^{-6}} = 1 - 7.44657 \times 10^{-3}
\]
\end{mdframed}

\noindent See Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $I = I$.

\subsection{General Case (unconstrained)}

When no constraint is applied between the systems, $k_d$ stays in its full form:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1}
\]
Both mass and radius must be specified independently for each system.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (General Case):} $S_1$: $M_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{20}$\,kg, $R_1 = 2.00000 \times 10^{-7}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_2 = 1.00000 \times 10^{7}$\,m.

Compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{(1.00000 \times 10^{20})(1.00000 \times 10^{7})}{(1.00000 \times 10^{30})(2.00000 \times 10^{-7})} = 5.00000 \times 10^{3}
\]

\textbf{GTD calculation:} Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation directly. Each system's Schwarzschild radius is $r_s = 2GM/c^2$:
\begin{align}
r_{s,1} &= \frac{2GM_1}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{20})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{m} \notag \\
r_{s,2} &= \frac{2GM_2}{c^2} = \frac{2(6.67430 \times 10^{-11})(1.00000 \times 10^{30})}{(2.99792 \times 10^{8})^2} \notag \\
&= 1.48523 \times 10^{3}\,\text{m} \notag
\end{align}
GTD for each system via $\sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$:
\begin{align}
\text{GTD}_1 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{-7}}{2.00000 \times 10^{-7}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 7.42617 \times 10^{-1}} = 1 - 4.92670 \times 10^{-1} \notag \\
\text{GTD}_2 &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,2}}{R_2}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{3}}{1.00000 \times 10^{7}}} \notag \\
&= \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-4}} = 1 - 7.42644 \times 10^{-5} \notag
\end{align}
giving the relative pace of time:
\[
\frac{\text{GTD}_1}{\text{GTD}_2} = \frac{1 - 4.92670 \times 10^{-1}}{1 - 7.42644 \times 10^{-5}} = 1 - 4.92632 \times 10^{-1}
\]
\end{mdframed}

\noindent See Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column General.

\subsection{Inertia--Time Dilation Identities}

The preceding cases derive time dilation from inertia. The relationship is invertible: given the compactness ratio and the radius ratio, the moment of inertia ratio is fully determined. From Equation~\ref{eq:kd}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:inertia_recovery}
\frac{I_1}{I_2} = k_d \cdot \left(\frac{R_1}{R_2}\right)^3
\end{equation}
The inertia ratio decomposes into a compactness ratio and a spatial scale factor (the volume ratio). This is the STE (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}) inverted: the $(R_1/R_2)^3$ factor converts the $R^{-1}$ dependence in DeGerlia compactness back to the $R^2$ dependence in inertia.
