\section{Introduction}
The Schwarzschild gravitational time-dilation calculation $\sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$ contains two radii: $R$, the radius of a system, and $r_s = 2GM/c^2$, the Schwarzschild radius of the same mass. Same mass at two different radii: the formula compares two systems. The notation places $r_s$ inside one symbol, which gives the formula a single-system appearance, but the calculation is two-system. The single-system reading gives the right number --- the formula carries both systems --- and is incoherent within relativity, where a frame of reference requires a comparator.

This paper writes both systems openly. For each uniform spherical system, the DeGerlia compactness $D = M/R$ (kg/m) is the mass-to-radius ratio \citep{degerlia202505}. The DeGerlia compactness ratio $k_d = D_1/D_2 = M_1 R_2/(M_2 R_1)$ governs the time dilation between two such systems. The static-mass case ($M_1 = M_2$, different radii) is the standard gravitational time-dilation calculation \citep{einstein1915,schwarzschild1916radius}. The static-radius case ($R_1 = R_2$, different masses) is the standard Lorentz time-dilation calculation \citep{einstein1905}. Six worked cases in Section~\ref{sec:expressions} and the appendix tables (Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}) tabulate the DeGerlia compactness ratio $k_d$ and the gravitational time-dilation calculation for each pair across the classical and relativistic regimes. Agreement is not asserted on trust: every input and output is tabulated in the appendix, and each row is reproducible directly from $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ and the standard Schwarzschild form.
\section{General Relativity and Compactness}

\subsection{Compactness}\label{sec:standard_compactness}
Compactness is the dimensionless ratio of a system's mass to its radius, scaled by $G/c^2$. The most common forms are $C = GM/(Rc^2)$ and $r_s/R = 2GM/(Rc^2) = 2C$, where $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild radius. Both measure how close a system sits to its Schwarzschild threshold: $r_s/R = 1$ marks the gravitational-collapse condition.

\subsection{Inertial Density $P$}\label{sec:inertial_density}
Inertial density $P$ \citep{degerlia202505} is the moment of inertia of a system divided by its volume:
\begin{equation}
P = I/V
\end{equation}
Since linear inertia is mass, this reduces directly to mass density $\rho = M/V$ in the linear case; inertial density is the rotational generalization of mass density, a quantity already familiar from linear mechanics. Or more accurately, mass density is simply the linear interpretation of inertial density. 

Substituting $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$ and $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$, with $R$ the radius, this reduces to:
\begin{equation}
P = \frac{M}{R} \times \frac{3}{10\pi}
\end{equation}
Just as ordinary density ($\rho = M/V$) is mass distributed over volume, inertial density is rotational inertia distributed over volume. As stated in the prior work: \textit{mass and density are to linear motion as moment of inertia and inertial density are to rotational motion.}

\subsection{DeGerlia Compactness $D$}
$D = M/R$ is a simplified analog to inertial density, related to $P$ by the geometric factor $3/10\pi$, and represents the mass over radius ratio for a system. Given that this quantity is almost always used in a ratio that would cancel out all geometric vectors and constants, for ease of use, this metric $D$ for inertial density is typically used over the geometrically accurate inertial density, $P$. 



\subsection{DeGerlia Threshold $D_{crit}$}

The Schwarzschild condition can be expressed as a static universal constant threshold of DeGerlia compactness $D$. There are several advantages to this approach, not the least of which is that you don't have to calculate the threshold for every mass. And, it's much easier to calculate $D$ than $P$. Inertial density is an intuitive metric that's easier to work with. 

Setting $R = r_s$ (the Schwarzschild condition) \citep{schwarzschild1916radius} and solving yields the \textbf{DeGerlia Threshold}: the universal constant of DeGerlia compactness at which the escape velocity equals the speed of light:
\begin{equation}
D_{crit} = \frac{c^2}{2G} = (6.73295 \pm 0.00015) \times 10^{26} \text{ kg/m}
\end{equation}
Note this value is exact; its precision is limited by $G = (6.67430 \pm 0.00015) \times 10^{-11}$ m$^3$\,kg$^{-1}$\,s$^{-2}$ \citep{codata2018}. 

\section{The Space-Time Equivalence}
The following identity relates the time-dilation ratio of two uniform spherical systems to their mass and radius.\footnote{This identity has been previously referred to as the ``space-time equivalence''~\cite{degerlia202501}.}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:kd}
k_d = \frac{I_1}{I_2} \cdot \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^3 = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1}
\end{equation}
where $I_1, I_2$ are the moments of inertia, $R_1, R_2$ are the radii, $M_1, M_2$ are the masses, and $D_i = M_i/R_i$ is the DeGerlia Compactness of system $i$. The three forms in Equation~\ref{eq:kd} are algebraically identical; $(I_1/I_2)(R_2/R_1)^3$ exposes the inertia-radius structure, $D_1/D_2$ exposes the compactness-ratio structure, and $M_1 R_2/(M_2 R_1)$ is the fully expanded form.

The pace of time has meaning only relative to another pace of time. The DeGerlia compactness ratio $k_d$ governs the relative time-dilation between two uniform spherical systems. The standard gravitational time-dilation calculation takes the Schwarzschild form $\sqrt{1 - k_s}$, where $k_s = D/D_{crit} = r_s/R$. The DeGerlia compactness ratio $k_d = D_1/D_2$ and the Schwarzschild form $k_s$ are the same scale factor $k$ evaluated by two routes; the subscript denotes which route produced it.

Via the Schwarzschild radius, the same scale factor is:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ks}
k_s = \frac{D}{D_{crit}} = \frac{r_s}{R}
\end{equation}
where $D = M/R$ is the system's DeGerlia Compactness and $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ is its Schwarzschild radius. $k_s$ is the Schwarzschild-route name for $k$ that appears in the standard $\sqrt{1 - k}$ for GTD.

\subsection{Relating Linear Scale Factor $k$ to General Relativity}
Time dilation is always:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:td_k}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - k}
\end{equation}
where $d\tau/dt$ is the time-dilation factor between the two systems and $k$ is the DeGerlia compactness ratio for the case being computed. The specific forms are:
\begin{itemize}
\item $k_d = D_1/D_2$ (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}) --- via inertial density ratio
\item $k_s = D/D_{crit} = r_s/R$ (Equation~\ref{eq:ks}) --- via Schwarzschild radius
\end{itemize}
Same $k$ symbol across forms: a given numerical $k$ produces the same time-scale-factor result regardless of which form it appears in.

\subsection{Classical Static Density Example}

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Setup.} A uniform sphere of mass $1$\,kg and radius $1$\,m (System~2) is scaled isometrically by a factor of two, producing a sphere of mass $2^3 = 8$\,kg and radius $2$\,m (System~1). The linear scale factor between them is $k = R_1/R_2 = 2$ by construction.

\textbf{Change in inertia.} The moments of inertia are
\[
I_1 = \tfrac{2}{5}(8)(2)^2 = 12.8\,\text{kg\,m}^2, \qquad
I_2 = \tfrac{2}{5}(1)(1)^2 = 0.4\,\text{kg\,m}^2,
\]
so $I_1/I_2 = 32 = 2^5 = k^5$ --- the moment of inertia changes by the fifth power of the linear scale factor.

\textbf{Gravitational time dilation.} Each system's Schwarzschild radius is $r_s = 2GM/c^2$, giving $r_{s,1} = 1.18819 \times 10^{-26}$\,m and $r_{s,2} = 1.48523 \times 10^{-27}$\,m. The Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation $gtd = \sqrt{1 - r_s/R}$ is then
\begin{gather*}
gtd_1 = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1}} = 1 - 2.97046 \times 10^{-27}, \\
gtd_2 = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_{s,2}}{R_2}} = 1 - 7.42616 \times 10^{-28},
\end{gather*}
so the relative time dilation between the two systems is
\[
\frac{gtd_1}{gtd_2} = 1 - 2.22785 \times 10^{-27}.
\]

\textbf{DeGerlia compactness ratio.} The ratio $k_d$ follows from the DeGerlia compactness $D = M/R$ ($D_1 = 4$\,kg/m, $D_2 = 1$\,kg/m):
\[
k_d = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{4}{1} = 4 = k^2, \qquad \sqrt{k_d} = 2 = k.
\]

This matches the Classical Static Density Example column of Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}: the gravitational time dilation ratio is $1 - 2.22785 \times 10^{-27}$ and $\sqrt{k_d} = 2$.

The moment of inertia changes by $2^5 = 32$; the relative time dilation $1 - 2.22785 \times 10^{-27}$ is obtained equivalently from the Schwarzschild and inertial-density formulations.}

\section{General and Special Relativity}
Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:schwarzschild}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{R}}
\end{equation}
where $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild radius and $R$ is the radius.

Lorentz time dilation:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:lorentz}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}
\end{equation}
where $v$ is the velocity of the moving frame and $c$ is the speed of light.

These are the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, where $k = r_s/R$ in the gravitational case and $k = v^2/c^2$ in the velocity case. The two expressions are equivalent: the escape velocity at radius $R$ is $v^2 = 2GM/R$ \citep{goldstein2002}, so $v^2/c^2 = 2GM/(Rc^2) = r_s/R$.

Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation is the STE (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}) evaluated under the constraint $M_1 = M_2$ (same mass, different radii). Lorentz time dilation is the STE evaluated under the constraint $R_1 = R_2$ (same radius, different masses). Both are cases of the same DeGerlia-compactness-based relation; the derivations of each are developed in Section~\ref{sec:expressions}.


\section{Expressions of Relative Inertial Linear Scale}\label{sec:expressions}

The universal scale factor between any two systems is the DeGerlia compactness ratio $k_d$ (Equation~\ref{eq:kd}), with $k_d = D_1/D_2 = M_1 R_2/(M_2 R_1)$. Each subsection below applies a specific physical constraint that simplifies $k_d$, derives the resulting form, and demonstrates the calculation with a worked numerical example.

Numerical values are reported at six significant figures in e-style notation. Reference constants used throughout: $D_{crit} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26}$ kg/m, $G = 6.67430 \times 10^{-11}$ m$^3$\,kg$^{-1}$\,s$^{-2}$, $c = 2.99792 \times 10^{8}$ m/s. Example parameter sets match the columns of the pair comparison tables (Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}).

\subsection{Static Mass ($M_1 = M_2$)}

When the two systems share the same mass, $k_d$ is the ratio of their DeGerlia compactness values:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{D_1}{D_2}
\]
Each system's normalized DeGerlia compactness against the DeGerlia Threshold yields $D/D_{crit} = r_s/R$, recovering the Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation parameter.

This case is the standard gravitational time-dilation calculation; the DeGerlia compactness ratio $D_1/D_2$ produces the familiar Schwarzschild factor here.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Static Mass):} System 1 is the system being measured; System 2 is the same mass at its Schwarzschild radius. $S_1$: $M_1 = 2.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_1 = 3.00000 \times 10^{3}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = M_1$, $R_2 = r_s = 2GM_1/c^2 = 2.97046 \times 10^{3}$\,m.

DeGerlia compactness for each system:
\[
D_1 = \frac{M_1}{R_1} = \frac{2.00000 \times 10^{30}}{3.00000 \times 10^{3}} = 6.66667 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg/m}
\]
\[
D_2 = \frac{M_2}{R_2} = \frac{2.00000 \times 10^{30}}{2.97046 \times 10^{3}} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg/m}
\]

Normalized to the DeGerlia Threshold:
\[
D_{norm,1} = \frac{D_1}{D_{crit}} = \frac{6.66667 \times 10^{26}}{6.73295 \times 10^{26}} = 9.90155 \times 10^{-1}
\]
\[
D_{norm,2} = \frac{D_2}{D_{crit}} = \frac{6.73295 \times 10^{26}}{6.73295 \times 10^{26}} = 1.00000
\]

DeGerlia compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{D_{norm,1}}{D_{norm,2}} = 9.90155 \times 10^{-1}
\]

Schwarzschild ratio:
\[
k_s = \frac{r_s}{R_1} = \frac{2.97046 \times 10^{3}}{3.00000 \times 10^{3}} = 9.90155 \times 10^{-1}
\]

GTD via $k_d$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_d} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{D_{norm,1}}{D_{norm,2}}} = 1 - 9.00780 \times 10^{-1}
\]

GTD via $k_s$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_s} = \sqrt{1 - 9.90155 \times 10^{-1}} = 1 - 9.00777 \times 10^{-1}
\]

This case is tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $M = M$ of Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}.
\end{mdframed}

\subsection{Static Radius ($R_1 = R_2$)}

When the two systems share the same radius, $k_d$ is the ratio of their DeGerlia compactness values:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{D_1}{D_2}
\]
The Lorentz factor (Equation~\ref{eq:lorentz}) has the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, with $k = v^2/c^2$. Since velocity is distance per unit time, $v^2/c^2$ is the square of a ratio of two lengths, with the shared time unit cancelling. Gravitational time dilation confirms this directly: $v^2/c^2 = r_s/R$ via the escape velocity identity, so the velocity-based $k$ of special relativity and the radius-based $k$ of general relativity are the same length ratio.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Static Radius):} System 1 is the system being measured; System 2 is at its Schwarzschild radius, defining the common radius. $S_1$: $M_1 = 2.00000 \times 10^{28}$\,kg, $R_1 = 2.97046 \times 10^{3}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = 2.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_2 = R_1 = r_s = 2GM_2/c^2 = 2.97046 \times 10^{3}$\,m.

DeGerlia compactness for each system:
\[
D_1 = \frac{M_1}{R_1} = \frac{2.00000 \times 10^{28}}{2.97046 \times 10^{3}} = 6.73295 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg/m}
\]
\[
D_2 = \frac{M_2}{R_2} = \frac{2.00000 \times 10^{30}}{2.97046 \times 10^{3}} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg/m}
\]

Normalized to the DeGerlia Threshold:
\[
D_{norm,1} = \frac{D_1}{D_{crit}} = \frac{6.73295 \times 10^{24}}{6.73295 \times 10^{26}} = 1.00000 \times 10^{-2}
\]
\[
D_{norm,2} = \frac{D_2}{D_{crit}} = \frac{6.73295 \times 10^{26}}{6.73295 \times 10^{26}} = 1.00000
\]

DeGerlia compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{D_{norm,1}}{D_{norm,2}} = 1.00000 \times 10^{-2}
\]

Schwarzschild ratio for System 1 ($r_{s,1} = 2GM_1/c^2 = 2.97046 \times 10^{1}$\,m):
\[
k_s = \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1} = \frac{2.97046 \times 10^{1}}{2.97046 \times 10^{3}} = 1.00000 \times 10^{-2}
\]

GTD via $k_d$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_d} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{D_{norm,1}}{D_{norm,2}}} = 1 - 5.01256 \times 10^{-3}
\]

GTD via $k_s$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_s} = \sqrt{1 - 1.00000 \times 10^{-2}} = 1 - 5.01256 \times 10^{-3}
\]

This case is tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $R = R$ of Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}.
\end{mdframed}

\subsection{Constant Density (Isometric) ($\rho_1 = \rho_2$)}

When the two systems share the same density, $M \propto R^3$, so $M_1/M_2 = (R_1/R_2)^3$. Substituting into $k_d$:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \left(\frac{R_1}{R_2}\right)^3 \cdot \frac{R_2}{R_1} = \left(\frac{R_1}{R_2}\right)^2
\]
The DeGerlia compactness ratio reduces to the square of the radius ratio. The linear scale factor follows as $\sqrt{k_d} = R_1/R_2$, recovering the isometric equivalence.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Constant Density):} System 1 is the system being measured; System 2 is the comparator at its Schwarzschild radius. $S_1$: $M_1 = 7.63040 \times 10^{28}$\,kg, $R_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{3}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = 2.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_2 = r_s = 2GM_2/c^2 = 2.97046 \times 10^{3}$\,m. Both at $\rho = 1.82181 \times 10^{19}$\,kg/m$^3$.

DeGerlia compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{(7.63040 \times 10^{28})(2.97046 \times 10^{3})}{(2.00000 \times 10^{30})(1.00000 \times 10^{3})} = 1.13327 \times 10^{-1}
\]

Schwarzschild ratio for System 1 ($r_{s,1} = 2GM_1/c^2 = 1.13327 \times 10^{2}$\,m):
\[
k_s = \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1} = \frac{1.13327 \times 10^{2}}{1.00000 \times 10^{3}} = 1.13327 \times 10^{-1}
\]

GTD via $k_d$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_d} = \sqrt{1 - 1.13327 \times 10^{-1}} = 1 - 5.83337 \times 10^{-2}
\]

GTD via $k_s$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_s} = \sqrt{1 - 1.13327 \times 10^{-1}} = 1 - 5.83337 \times 10^{-2}
\]

This case is tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $\rho = \rho$ of Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}.
\end{mdframed}

\subsection{Constant Inertia ($I_1 = I_2$)}

When the two systems share the same moment of inertia, $M_1 R_1^2 = M_2 R_2^2$, so $M_1/M_2 = (R_2/R_1)^2$. Substituting into $k_d$:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{R_2}{R_1} = \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^3
\]
The DeGerlia compactness ratio reduces to the cube of the radius ratio.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (Constant Inertia):} System 1 is the system being measured; System 2 is the comparator at its Schwarzschild radius. $S_1$: $M_1 = 1.76450 \times 10^{29}$\,kg, $R_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{4}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = 2.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_2 = r_s = 2GM_2/c^2 = 2.97046 \times 10^{3}$\,m. Both have $I = MR^2 = 1.76450 \times 10^{37}$\,kg$\cdot$m$^2$.

DeGerlia compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{(1.76450 \times 10^{29})(2.97046 \times 10^{3})}{(2.00000 \times 10^{30})(1.00000 \times 10^{4})} = 2.62034 \times 10^{-2}
\]

Schwarzschild ratio for System 1 ($r_{s,1} = 2GM_1/c^2 = 2.62034 \times 10^{2}$\,m):
\[
k_s = \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1} = \frac{2.62034 \times 10^{2}}{1.00000 \times 10^{4}} = 2.62034 \times 10^{-2}
\]

GTD via $k_d$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_d} = \sqrt{1 - 2.62034 \times 10^{-2}} = 1 - 1.31836 \times 10^{-2}
\]

GTD via $k_s$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_s} = \sqrt{1 - 2.62034 \times 10^{-2}} = 1 - 1.31836 \times 10^{-2}
\]

This case is tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column $I = I$ of Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}.
\end{mdframed}

\subsection{General Case (unconstrained)}

When no constraint is applied between the systems, $k_d$ stays in its full form:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1}
\]
Both mass and radius must be specified independently for each system.

\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Example (General Case):} System 1 is the system being measured; System 2 is the comparator at its Schwarzschild radius. $S_1$: $M_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{29}$\,kg, $R_1 = 1.00000 \times 10^{4}$\,m. $S_2$: $M_2 = 2.00000 \times 10^{30}$\,kg, $R_2 = r_s = 2GM_2/c^2 = 2.97046 \times 10^{3}$\,m.

DeGerlia compactness ratio:
\[
k_d = \frac{M_1 R_2}{M_2 R_1} = \frac{(1.00000 \times 10^{29})(2.97046 \times 10^{3})}{(2.00000 \times 10^{30})(1.00000 \times 10^{4})} = 1.48523 \times 10^{-2}
\]

Schwarzschild ratio for System 1 ($r_{s,1} = 2GM_1/c^2 = 1.48523 \times 10^{2}$\,m):
\[
k_s = \frac{r_{s,1}}{R_1} = \frac{1.48523 \times 10^{2}}{1.00000 \times 10^{4}} = 1.48523 \times 10^{-2}
\]

GTD via $k_d$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_d} = \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-2}} = 1 - 7.45394 \times 10^{-3}
\]

GTD via $k_s$:
\[
\sqrt{1 - k_s} = \sqrt{1 - 1.48523 \times 10^{-2}} = 1 - 7.45394 \times 10^{-3}
\]

This case is tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:pair_ratios}, column General of Appendix~\ref{app:pair_table}.
\end{mdframed}
